How might we help tourists and locals explore and interact with their surroundings in a fun and educative way?

A scavenger hunt app which enables its users to take part in ready-made or customisable hunts for real location near them and interact with these locations through a variety of challenges.

Role: All elements of UX (focus) and UI

Duration: October 2018 to May 2019

Research

Affinity Map

After conducting some user interviews, I compiled the data from those interviews into an affinity map. From this, I was able to extract the following user insights.

Interaction

When I would play Pokemon Go, I wouldn’t engage with my surroundings.

When they’d be a pokemon nearby [in Pokemon Go], everyone would just stare at their phones.

Pokemon Go created a bridge between the real and virtual world, but the experience is wholly through your phone.

Users want activities to extend beyond their screen. They want to interact with their surroundings and with their friends as they find the experience enjoyable and rewarding.

Authentic, Local Experience

I like going to places that locals would go to.

[In Pokemon Go], they wouldn’t bring you to interesting/unique places.

I always want to feel like a local but it’s not easy if you don’t know anyone.

Users want to visit local, interesting and unique locations. They want to have an authentic experience of the places they live in/travel to, in search for a more in depth understanding of those places.

Design Pattern

When creating Qwest’s wireframes, I used various design patterns.

For instance, I designed a search bar with autocomplete to help users search faster for a European city with hunts. I kept the suggestion list short so as to avoid overwhelming users, which was easy since not every city in Europe will have hunts. I also styled the entered and suggested terms differently in order to highlight their differences and made the suggested terms bolder to draw the users’ eyes to them since users already know what they have entered themselves.

On the search results page, I designed actionable search results to allow users to perform certain tasks - saving, sharing and downloading - on hunts they were looking for without leaving the results page.

I used the design pattern of progressive disclosure to break down the advanced task of customising a hunt into under-standable smaller tasks in order to avoid overwhelming/confusing users.

Usability Tests

New Design Iteration

I conducted some usability tests to test my mid-fidelity prototype. I analysed and reported my test findings on a rainbow spreadsheet. I then determined which errors in my interactive prototype were most critical and prioritised fixes.

Problem #1

To most test participants it was not clear that the explanation for the search feature in the onboarding was being typed in the search bar. This was mostly due to the fact that I had not properly achieved the “typing” animation. However, this made me realised that my “original” animation was not just confusing but was also in-consistent with the rest of the onboarding which consisted of coach marks.

Problem #2

I had not included any explanations for the “active hunt” button into the onboarding because I wanted to highlight only the two main features of the app - the search and customising features - but this turned out to be a mistake. Some test participants pointed out that there was no explanation for this button and none of them could figure out what it was for.

First solution to problem #1 and #2

My first solution to this problem was to redesign the onboarding so that the design was more consistent and included an explanation for the “Active hunt” button, which I also renamed “Ongoing hunt”. However, I was not very satisfied with this solution as I couldn’t ignore one of the test participants comment about how coach marks were not very useful, at least for the search bar and the “create hunt” button as both these elements were self-explanatory.

Final solution to problem #1 and #2

Instead of using coach marks, I decided to design a slide-through onboarding which highlights the main features of the app like I wanted originally. With this design, I was also able to include the sign up/log in feature which I had forgotten to include in my previous designs. As for an explanation for the “Ongoing hunt” button, I used the empty state of the screen to explain its purpose to new users.

Conclusion

I learnt a great deal whilst working on this app as this was my first UX project that I worked on as part Careerfoundry’s UX Design Course. I learnt how vital user research is as it helps place users at the centre of the project and provides the foundation for the initial design. In retrospect, I wish I had interviewed a few more people and taken the time to collect some quantitative data through a survey. This would have enabled me to support my interview findings on a larger sample. When it came to the design, getting feedback from my mentor and friends made me open and receptive to criticism. I actually enjoyed when I was being told that I should change something, improve my design in some way. I could say that the biggest lesson I learnt during this project was to not be too attached to a particular design as well as afraid to start over.
